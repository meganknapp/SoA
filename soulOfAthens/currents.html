Currents

Currents community sprouted from a discussion group among members of a consumer coop movement in Dayton Ohio in 1971. Ten years later, Roger Wilkens, Marty Zinn, Pete Hill, Eddie Kruse and Bob Henninge acquired 163 acres of rolling farmland in Glouster, Ohio and the dream took root.

Members immediately began the work of renovating the old farmhouse and building a community based on co-operativism and social activism.

Organizing principle

The group envisioned Currents as a rural community where they could live out their shared values of equality, fairness, appropriate land use, and independent energy sources. All were social change activists and had eclectic skills to use at Currents, as well as the larger community of Athens County. Over the years members of Currents provided training to a dozen new worker cooperatives, they started and/or led a variety of social change organizations, such as Appalachian Center for Economic Networks, River Valley Community School, Buckeye forest Council, and the Center for the Creation of Cooperation.

Gathering for brunch

On any given Sunday, you’ll find some of the community, now 28 people, together for brunch in the original farmhouse, catching up with one another, enjoying a nice fire in the winter months and sharing delicious homemade food. This tradition also gives them a chance to discuss pending business in a casual way. Conversations run the gamut from the kids’ schooling to future plans for the day or goals further down the road. Certainly, politics and current affairs is a topic of interest to all, and unlike most families they seem to agree!

Transition of power

The members of the community own a share in the land, but no single person owns the house they live in. Currents is a non-profit organization and the land is owned by that entity. The governing style of Currents is “democratic and egalitarian” according to Laura Rose, one of the steerers, or managers of the community. The steerer role rotates through the group twice a year.

Laura joined Currents in 2009, and exemplifies the transition that Currents is making to bring in younger members as part of the leadership of the community.

Homeschooling children

Laura and her husband Joe Hazelbaker are raising 4 children at Currents. Laura opines that living in a cooperative society is important, “I want my kids to have hope to overcome these huge difficulties we’re facing in the world, so the first part of their lives are filled with happiness and getting to know the land, loving the land, seeing how food is grown and learning real skills.”

Besides academic schoolwork, Laura’s children also have chores helping with the animals and plants. She takes them on adventures hiking through the forest that surrounds the farm and she and veteran maple tapper Bob Henninge teach them how to drill the holes and place the taps.

On a recent field trip into the maple grove the kids collected buckets of tapped raw sap to be boiled for hours into maple syrup. While the kids did collect an untold amount directly into their mouths, the community was able to store a dozen or so jars of the sweet sap for use during the year.

Laura, her husband Joe and their children embody the community’s positive focus on intergenerational co-operation and contribute to the hope for a healthy and long-lived home for everyone at Currents.
