SuBAMUH

In a warm voice, jolly-faced Jan Griesinger welcomes us to her room. She is currently living in one of Athen’s assisted living centers for the elderly. Jan was once a woman's right activist and one of the founding members of Subamah-a women safe space center. Subamah derives its name as a form of abbreviation from Susan B. Anthony Memorial Hill Unrest Home. Jan narrates the times they had back in the days with fond memories of gardening; remarkably the strawberry patch, grape vine and the small garden of vegetables. The beauty of the scenery out in the woods , the parties, the times spent together as a community in the residential.

Land Agreements

Charlene Suggs, a contracts expert from the Wisteria community group is helping them draw up their land agreement. Charlene pointed out that of the 100 communities that intend to form, 99% fail. Part of being intentional about forming a community is getting agreements about how land ownership will be managed, what the guiding principal of a community will be, and how decisions will be made. Having these legal agreements is the insurance that, whatever challenges the community faces in the future, they have the means to solve.

Goat Farming

Besides anarchy, permaculture and goat farming are at the top of Black Locust’s community plan. Badger Johnson is a proponent of controlled burns on farmland, which was a part of the First Nation peoples measures to deal with invasive species like multi-flora rose and Japanese honeysuckle, which compete with native grasses. He has introduced neighboring farms and nature preserves to the usefulness of fire in preparing and renewing land.

Sasha’s goat herd has doubled in the last year. She raises the rare heritage British Guernsey for milk and has also crossbred a Savannah buck with the Guernsey mama for her meat herd. The hilly property, covered with brambles and grass, is perfect “goat food.” Three baby goats have been born this season so far.

The Next Generation

The other young’un on the farm is nine-year-old Mirna, who attends an alternative grade school in Athens called Solid Ground, she also pitches in to help Sasha with the care of the goats and the Great Pyrenees who guard everyone who lives at Black Locust. When Sasha envisions the future of Black Locust collective, she sees an Eden of sorts, “a garden of food, animals and people.”

The collective intends to create a rural space for like-minded people, especially millennials who Badger describes as generally having fewer financial resources and being more mobile than previous generations.
