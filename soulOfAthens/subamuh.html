SuBAMUH

Susan B. Anthony Memorial Hill Unrest Home, was for many years a dream come true for Mary Morgan and Jan Griessinger, who envisioned a rural sanctuary for women completely free of men. A beautiful and safe environment for women to retreat from a world of sexism, violence and inequality to a world of beauty, fruitful strawberry patches, verdant scenery and feminist philosophy. Gardening, practical skill-building workshops and pond-side parties with women-lead programs filled their days.

Organizing Principle: Feminism (the F Word)

Jan and Mary met in a women’s liberation group in Dayton, Ohio. In 19XX when Jan and Mary began their adventure in intentional community on 157 acres of land in the Athens hills, feminist groups were an important part of a changing American culture and a challenge to the prevalent patriarchy. Subamah was based on the principles of what Jan calls “the F word- feminism". To sustain the community, a collective leadership module was used. The ty was a non-profit corporation with communal governing measures. They had bi-weekly meetings to sort out tasks and allocate responsibilities since they had to maintain the Subamah estate by themselves.

Separatism

In Subamah’s prime years it provided residencies for many women, feminist-centered education and lively gatherings, providing scenic camping grounds, retreats and emotional safe space for women in the country.

“Our goal was a women only space to have a safe space, safe rural space where women could live, and come and visit. So if that was considered separatist. Yes.”

Misfortunes

Some believe it was this hardline position that weakened Subamuh’s potential for survival.

If a same-sex couple member of the community had a boy child, for instance, they had to leave the community before the boy reached the age of 8. This kind of inflexibility led to some of early members leaving the community. Both Mary and Jan had to eventually leave the community due to failing health, and the lack of support systems nearby.

Future Hopes

Jan recognises that the needs for women safe spaces might not be as urgent as it was in the past, times have changed. She hopes the land will be used as a womens land trust.

The Subamah property currently hosts a single LGBTQ residency. In the backyard you can still see the campground with wood stacked for bonfires, hardware tools rusting in the shed, old chalkboard welcome signs, and empty chairs put away.

Though this community has now fallen silent, this was once a refuge for fearless women, brimming with activism and hope.
